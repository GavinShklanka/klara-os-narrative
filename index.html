The Problem — Two Intersecting Failures

Navigation fails.
Then access fails.

Nova Scotia's healthcare system is experiencing two compounding failures. Neither can be solved by adding capacity to an already-strained system. Both require a new intelligence layer.

Failure 01 · Navigation
Patients don't know where to go
No system coordinates the care options that already exist
The gap
Pharmacies can prescribe for 19+ minor ailments. VirtualCareNS serves 150–200 patients daily but turns away 100–150 more. 811 provides nurse triage 24/7. No layer connects patients to these options before they default to the emergency department.
The result
181,547 CTAS 4–5 ED visits annually. 52,521 LWBS events carrying documented 14% higher 7-day mortality risk. ED cost per visit: $275–$355 versus $30–$110 for pharmacy or virtual care.
Infrastructure status
~$470M invested in digital health. OPOR ($412.7M), VirtualCareNS, Novari ($17M/5yr), DIS/SHARE. All provider-facing. None answers the patient's question: "Where should I go?"
Failure 02 · Physical Access
Patients can't physically reach care
Even when the right pathway exists — rural patients cannot reach it
The gap
51,552 rural ED closure hours in 2023–24 (55% unplanned). Roseway Hospital alone: 4,832 hours — 201 full days. When a facility closes, no system coordinates alternative delivery. Patients drive 60–120+ minutes or forgo care entirely.
The scale
66,768 unattached patients. No provincial system tracks where patients seek care and fail. No demand-side intelligence exists to inform rural physician deployment planning.
What's missing
OPOR knows what happened to a patient after they received care. KLARA will know where patients are trying to access care and failing. That demand-side intelligence is the foundation of Solution 2.

Core System Pipeline

How KLARA routes
every patient.

Seven deterministic modules. Each receives the NavigationContext, performs a single transformation, and returns an enriched context. Two-second timeout per module with AMBER/811 fallback — no patient is ever left without a pathway.

M1
Intake Validation
Field validation. UTF-8 normalization. SNOMED-CT deduplication. DIS medication complexity flag for auth users (≥5 active = comorbidity, +10 risk uplift).
›
M2
Symptom Parsing
Symptom graph construction. SNOMED-CT hierarchy mapping. Negation resolution. Co-occurrence detection. MedLine-informed urgency weighting.
›
M3
Risk Classification
GREEN/AMBER/RED. Schmitt-Thompson protocol alignment. Asymmetric thresholds: pediatric, geriatric, and rural populations. Critical Symptom Register: mandatory ED override.
›
M4
Context Enrichment
DIS/SHARE medication query (consent-gated). Service directory including NSH-registered delivery points. Closure feeds. Drive-time estimation. Temporal demand model.
›
M5
Routing & Access
Pathway scoring: 40% acuity + 25% availability + 20% proximity + 15% preference. ACCESS BARRIER DETECTION — evaluates access_barrier_flag. If TRUE: routes to Sol. 2.
›
M6
Recommendation
RAG-powered output. Sol. 1: pathway + rationale + directions. Sol. 2: practitioner deployment details + delivery point + transit coordination. Non-diagnostic language enforced.
›
M7
Structured Output
FHIR R4 Composition. CA Core+ v0.3.0 validated. Sol. 2 adds: ServiceRequest, Location, and Appointment resources. AES-256 encryption. Patient HTML. Clinician summary.
Two-Phase Solution Architecture

Find the right pathway.
Then deliver it.

Solution 1 and Solution 2 share the same middleware pipeline, NavigationContext schema, and OPOR integration. They are sequential phases of the same patient journey — not separate systems.

Solution 1 · The Brain & Solution 2 · Physician Access
Solution 1 · The Brain
Route patients to the right pathway
KLARA is powered by an agentic AI engine, deployed through the user experience as the conversational agent Klara — with LangChain/LangGraph capacity and Gurobi-driven optimization that is already transferable to industry.
Problem solved
Navigation fails first: patients don't know which care service is appropriate — geographically available, open when needed, and within their mobility constraints. Solution 1 directly addresses this by routing every patient to the right pathway.
Agentic AI · Klara
The conversational agent Klara is the user-facing deployment of the agentic RAG engine (LangGraph DAG: INTAKE → HITL_GATE → CLARIFY → RISK_SCORE → ROUTE_PREP). It uses navigation language only, never diagnostic framing, and produces the PatientInputRecord that drives both optimizers.
Optimization · NS ecosystem
Powered by the middleware engine and supported analytically through Gurobi-driven optimisation, allowing Nova Scotians the proper insights to route them to accessible care. LP: x_ij ∈ {0,1}, minimize system strain + mismatch + wait. Constraints: one-assignment, capacity, clinical safety, closed facility exclusion, rural SLA.
Solver
PuLP (hackathon prototype) / Gurobi (production). Target: <500ms solve for 50 concurrent sessions.
Pathways
VirtualCareNS · Pharmacy (19+ minor ailments) · 811 Telehealth · Walk-in · Urgent Treatment Centre · Emergency Department
Value model
Moderate scenario (3% CTAS 4–5): ~$1.5M ED deflection value. Total modeled system efficiency: ~$8.7M annually. All projections conservative and explicitly modeled.
Solution 2 · Physician Access Optimization
Deliver care when it cannot be reached
When patients in rural or underserved regions cannot physically reach a recommended pathway, KLARA routes accredited physicians and nurse practitioners (NPs) to NSH-registered delivery points — community health centres, registered homes, telehealth stations — so those patients receive care where they are.
Who we route to
Patients in rural or underserved regions who cannot reach the recommended pathway: >60 min from service, mobility constraint, or nearest ED closed in a rural zone. access_barrier_flag = TRUE triggers Solution 2. Accredited physicians and NPs are then routed to NSH-registered delivery points (community health centres, registered homes, community spaces, telehealth stations, mobile units) so those patients get care without prohibitive travel.
Optimization
VRPTW: y_pk (practitioner → delivery point), z_ik (patient → point). Minimize: practitioner travel + unmet demand penalty + patient travel × vulnerability_weight. Constraints: capacity, time windows, regulatory compliance, clinical matching, geographic feasibility.
Solver
Gurobi (Toth & Vigo 2014 framework). <5s daily schedule. <500ms single-patient match. PuLP heuristic for hackathon prototype.
Delivery points
NSH-registered: community health centres, registered homes, community spaces, telehealth stations, mobile clinical units.
Demand signal
Every flagged session generates rural demand data that doesn't exist anywhere in the current provincial system. KLARA is the first system to capture where patients seek care and fail.
Bridged Solution
Solution 1 and Solution 2 operate together in sync — aligned with the digital infrastructure routing lapse and the rural specialized care plan. They perform physically as one integrated system: Sol. 1 routes patients to the right pathway; when access barriers are detected, Sol. 2 deploys practitioners to NSH-registered delivery points. Together they provide users a real-time solution that mitigates geographical strain and surfaces the resources available for each patient. This is not a third solution — it is Sol. 1 and Sol. 2 coming together to deliver the complete care pathway.

System Architecture — 8-Layer Design

Four layers.
One operating system.

KLARA OS is not an application — it is middleware that integrates with every major component of Nova Scotia's healthcare infrastructure. Each layer is independently scalable and replaceable without disrupting the system.

L1
User Interaction Layer — Citizens · Clinicians · Administrators
Interface Surface · YourHealthNS · 811 Voice · Web Portal · 2FA Authentication
YourHealthNS App Web Portal 811 Voice Integration Provincial Patient Registry 2FA (SMS/Email OTP) JWT Session Tokens (15-min expiry) WCAG 2.1 AA

Patient-facing intake via three channels — YourHealthNS mobile application, web portal, and 811 digital pathway. The 811 voice pathway is critical for rural and elderly demographics. The Province's discontinued MyHealthNS portal (shut down 2020 due to low uptake) is instructive: app-only strategies fail when they ignore demographic access constraints. KLARA is a multi-channel system from inception.

Authentication supports anonymous sessions (full navigation, no OPOR data pull) and authenticated returning users. authenticated sessions require Health Card + 2FA verification against the Provincial Patient Registry, enabling DIS medication pulls and OPOR history enrichment — both required for Solution 2 practitioner-to-patient matching. No PHI persists beyond the 15-minute session window.

L2
Conversational Intelligence Engine — Symptom Parsing · Risk Scoring · RAG Reasoning
Core Intelligence · LangGraph · SNOMED-CT · Schmitt-Thompson · MedLine API
Conversational agent Klara LangGraph RAG Agent SNOMED-CT (Canadian edition) Schmitt-Thompson Protocol MedLine/PubMed API ChromaDB Vector Store Redis Session Cache (15-min TTL, AES-256) Non-Diagnostic Constraint (RULE-RC-01)

A multi-turn agentic RAG system (LangGraph directed acyclic graph) that conducts conversational intake, extracts structured clinical data, and produces the PatientInputRecord driving both optimizers. Five-node graph: INTAKE → HITL_GATE (Critical Symptom Register check — any match exits immediately to 911/ED) → CLARIFY (max 3 turns, MedLine-grounded) → RISK_SCORE → ROUTE_PREP.

RAG-constrained architecture validated at 96.4% accuracy with zero hallucinations (npj Digital Medicine, 2025). All outputs use navigation language — never diagnostic framing. RULE-RC-01 is architecturally enforced at the governance layer and cannot be suppressed by operator configuration, user input, or system degradation. Health Canada SaMD non-classification maintained (December 2019 guidance).

The 15-minute Redis session cache is critical for Solution 2: practitioner matching requires the complete clinical profile generated during conversational intake. A sore throat that routes to pharmacy in Solution 1 may require a physician encounter in Solution 2 if DIS reveals a complex medication profile — the cache bridges both decisions with the same intake data.

L3–L4
System Integration & Middleware — FHIR R4 · OPOR · DIS/SHARE · 7-Module Pipeline
Integration Fabric · Oracle Health · VirtualCareNS · Novari · 2-Second Module Timeout
OPOR (Oracle Health Millennium) FHIR R4 / SMART on FHIR CDS Hooks VirtualCareNS (Maple) API DIS/SHARE (consent-gated) Novari eRequest Virtual Hallway 7-Module Pipeline AMBER/811 Fallback

The connective tissue between KLARA and Nova Scotia's existing digital health ecosystem. KLARA does not replace any provincial system — it orchestrates them. The 7-module middleware pipeline executes sequentially with 2-second timeouts per module and AMBER/811 fallback on failure. No patient is ever left without a care pathway regardless of system degradation.

OPOR integration (Oracle Health Millennium, FHIR R4) is bidirectional: OPOR feeds patient clinical context into KLARA for enriched routing; KLARA returns structured FHIR R4 Composition documents so clinicians receive organized intake data on first patient encounter. Novari eRequest receives pre-qualified structured referrals, reducing rejection and rework. DIS/SHARE medication data is queried only with explicit patient consent — flags medication complexity for risk uplift and practitioner matching.

L5a · L5b
Optimization Layer — Provincial Health Infrastructure · Care Delivery Endpoints
Sol. 1 LP · Sol. 2 VRPTW · Regulatory-Compliant Delivery Points · Practitioner Registry
Solution 1 — PuLP / Gurobi LP Solution 2 — Gurobi VRPTW Emergency Departments Urgent Treatment Centres VirtualCareNS Pharmacy (19+ minor ailments) NSH-Registered Delivery Points Freelance Practitioner Registry Transit Coordination

Both optimizers write to the same NavigationContext.optimization_result field. The downstream Recommendation (M6) and Output (M7) modules handle both solution types transparently. Solution 1 activates by default — LP assignment minimizes total system strain across available pathways. Solution 2 activates when access_barrier_flag = TRUE — VRPTW routes accredited practitioners to NSH-registered delivery points where patients can receive care without prohibitive travel.

Solution 2's optimization leverages a proven operations research framework (Toth & Vigo, 2014) applied to a novel context: instead of vehicles visiting customer nodes, licensed physicians and NPs visit regulatory-compliant delivery points — community health centres, registered homes, community spaces, telehealth stations, and mobile clinical units. The practitioner marketplace model requires no new permanent rural infrastructure. If Solution 2 fails, the system falls back to Solution 1's best available pathway with a notification that rural delivery coordination is temporarily unavailable.

Why KLARA is an Operating System, Not an Application
A typical healthcare application solves one problem for one user type. KLARA OS solves a systemic problem for the entire provincial health system — orchestrating multiple underlying platforms, managing real-time state across the care network, and serving as the intelligent coordination layer between citizens and healthcare infrastructure. It is not owned by a single department. It is not a portal. It is not a chatbot. It is provincial health infrastructure.

OPOR Bridge Architecture

$412.7M in infrastructure.
Activated for patients.

KLARA extends OPOR from clinical records into active care navigation and physician deployment. This is the core strategic argument for KLARA's value as provincial healthcare infrastructure.

What OPOR provides
The Clinical Backbone
Oracle Health Millennium platform. Single longitudinal health record for every Nova Scotian. FHIR R4 APIs, SMART on FHIR app launch, CDS Hooks for real-time clinical decision support. Provincial Patient Registry for identity resolution. IWK Health live December 2025. Central Zone targeting May 2026. Estimated lifecycle investment: $412.7M.
What OPOR does not do
The Remaining Gap
OPOR is clinician-facing. It does not help patients find available care. It does not coordinate care delivery to underserved communities. It does not generate demand-side intelligence about where patients seek care and fail. It provides no navigation layer between a patient's first symptom and their first clinical encounter. This gap exists across all five major provincial digital health investments.
What KLARA adds
The Bidirectional Bridge
OPOR → KLARA: patient identity, clinical history, medication profiles, allergy data — pre-populates intake for returning users. KLARA → OPOR: FHIR R4 structured intake documents, Solution 2 encounter records, and demand-side intelligence: where patients seek care and fail. Data that currently does not exist anywhere in the Nova Scotia provincial system. This feedback loop makes KLARA a force multiplier for the $412.7M OPOR investment.

Evidence Foundation — 24-Source Research Docket

The research supports
every design decision.

The KLARA OS architecture is grounded in peer-reviewed literature, CIHI data, and provincial government reports. All major design decisions have a documented evidence basis — validated at HIGH confidence against authoritative sources.

Digital Triage Trajectory
Semigran et al. BMJ 2015 → npj Digital Medicine 2025
AI triage accuracy has progressed from 57.7% correct triage (23 rule-based symptom checkers) to 96.4% accuracy with zero hallucinations in RAG-augmented systems. This trajectory directly validates KLARA's architectural choice: RAG-constrained conversational assessment is both the most accurate and the safest available approach for clinical navigation at scale.
Navigation System Impact
Baier et al. Health Policy 2019 · Turner et al. BMJ Open 2013
Cross-national analysis (6 healthcare systems) found structured navigation consistently achieves 15–25% lower non-urgent ED utilization. NHS 111 evidence demonstrates navigation improves routing quality even when total volumes remain stable — shaping KLARA's value framing around routing efficiency and access improvement rather than deflection volume alone.
VRPTW Optimization
Toth & Vigo (2014) · Fikar & Hirsch (2017) · Cherkesly et al. (2019)
Vehicle Routing Problem with Time Windows is well-established in operations research with documented healthcare applications including home health routing and community health worker deployment. Gurobi solves instances of Solution 2's scale (dozens of practitioners, hundreds of delivery points) within seconds. The NS rural physician deployment application is novel but built on proven technique.
LWBS Clinical Harm
McNaughton et al. JACEP Open 2024 · PMC 7972384 (2021)
LWBS patients face 14% higher relative risk of 7-day death or hospitalization (McNaughton 2024). 33.5% return within 30 days; 11.5–23.2% require admission on return (PMC 7972384). Nova Scotia's 52,521 annual LWBS events represent quantifiable clinical harm — not an administrative metric — making LWBS reduction a direct patient safety intervention.
Pharmacy & Virtual Care Pathways
Lapoint & Taylor JAPHA 2021 · Mehrotra et al. Health Affairs 2016
Pharmacy care for minor ailments consistently reduces unnecessary ED visits across jurisdictions — safety comparable to physician care for targeted conditions. NS pharmacists can prescribe for 19+ conditions but most patients are unaware. Telemedicine reduces ED visits in rural areas where primary care is limited (Mehrotra 2016) — supporting KLARA's VirtualCareNS and pharmacy routing pathways.
Non-SaMD Classification
Health Canada SaMD Guidance, December 2019
KLARA maintains non-SaMD classification under Health Canada's four exclusion criteria by using navigation language exclusively — never diagnostic language. RULE-RC-01 is architecturally enforced in the governance layer and cannot be suppressed by operator configuration or user input. Emergency indicators always override the routing system and cannot be disabled.
